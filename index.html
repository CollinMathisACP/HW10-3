4 Elements of a Distribution

First, a Definition:

The distribution is a visualization of how values occur relative to each other. It can be used to identify outliers and see how much the data varies, among other things.

Level

The level, or center, of a distribution can tell us a lot about data as a whole. The mode for example can help us see an unusually recurring value, which could be cause for further research. The mean and the median can give us an understanding of the data set’s middle values, and they can do even more when compared to each other. If the mean of a data set is much greater or lesser than the median, that could be an indication that there are outliers in either direction.

Spread

The spread of a distribution is the degree to which the data deviates from some middle value. There are multiple measurements that are relevant to this. The most important is standard deviation. In short, the standard deviation measures the average distance between the data points and the mean. A low value indicates that most of the data points are close to the mean. A distribution with a low standard deviation would appear to have data points clumped around a single central value. Furthermore, standard deviation can be used to calculate a z-score. The z-score is a standardized value that measures how many standard deviations a data point is away from the mean, in either the positive or negative direction.

Shape

The shape of a distribution is the first thing one notices when looking at a histogram. Simply, it is the appearance of the data points as a whole. This has multiple attributes. For example, it can have a skew, that is, a cluster of data points on one end of the scale and then other data points that go towards the opposite end of the scale. One can also look for peaks, locations along the scale that have a high frequency of values.

Outliers

Outliers can have many effects on a data set. First, it is important to understand resistant and nonresistant measures. The former, such as the mean, are heavily affected by outliers. The latter, such as the median, experience little to no effect. One data visualization that is particularly good at handling outliers is the box plot. In this chart, the 1st and 3rd quartiles are marked, as well as the median. This gives the reader a solid idea of the data’s center. It also displays outliers as dots on the far ends of the scale. This lets the reader see and interpret the presence of outliers without them affecting the rest of the data.
